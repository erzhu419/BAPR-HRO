\begin{abstract}
Stochastic hyperpath algorithms compute sets of alternative connections at each transit stop to minimize expected arrival time under delay uncertainty. However, these alternatives are ranked using historical delay distributions, which become stale when disruptions occur in real-time. We propose a Bandit-guided Hyperpath Selection (BHS) method that maintains the hyperpath structure while dynamically re-ranking alternatives using online learning from real-time delay observations. At each transfer point, a Lower Confidence Bound (LCB) policy---adapted from robust reinforcement learning---penalizes routes with high observed delays or cancellations, directing passengers toward reliable alternatives without recomputing the hyperpath. On synthetic transit networks with corridor disruptions, BHS reduces mean travel time by 12--16\% and worst-case (95th percentile) travel time by 42\% compared to static hyperpath following, with zero additional route computation. We further show that a neural value surrogate can accelerate the underlying hyperpath computation by 224$\times$ (from 816ms to 0.6ms per query) with 0.999 correlation to exact solutions, enabling real-time deployment on large networks.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Journey planning in public transit networks is inherently stochastic: vehicles may be delayed, canceled, or rerouted due to traffic, weather, or operational issues. Classical routing algorithms return a single fastest path based on scheduled timetables, ignoring this uncertainty. Stochastic approaches \citep{durner2024} address this by computing \emph{hyperpaths}---sets of alternative connections at each intermediate stop, ranked by expected destination arrival time. A passenger following a hyperpath boards whichever recommended bus arrives first, naturally hedging against individual connection failures.

However, existing stochastic routing assumes that delay distributions are \emph{stationary}: estimated once from historical data and held fixed throughout the journey. This assumption breaks during real-time disruptions. Consider a concrete scenario: a passenger plans to take bus 402 (direct route). Line 402 is unexpectedly canceled. The hyperpath includes fallback alternatives (lines 102, 311, 317), but their \emph{ranking}---computed under normal conditions---may no longer reflect reality. Line 311, ranked second under normal delays, may also be disrupted because it shares infrastructure with line 402. Meanwhile, line 317, ranked lower, may be unaffected and the best current option.

The key insight of this paper is that the \emph{structure} of the stochastic hyperpath (which alternatives exist) is robust to disruptions, but the \emph{ranking} of alternatives becomes stale. Rather than recomputing the entire hyperpath under estimated disruption distributions---which we show can be counter-productive due to over-conservative rerouting---we propose to \emph{learn} the correct ranking online from real-time delay and cancellation observations.

We formalize this as a contextual bandit problem at each transfer point:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Arms}: the available routes in the hyperpath at the current stop
    \item \textbf{Context}: current time, stop features, observed delays
    \item \textbf{Reward}: negative arrival time at destination (lower is better)
    \item \textbf{Policy}: Lower Confidence Bound (LCB) that penalizes uncertain/disrupted routes
\end{itemize}

The LCB policy is directly inspired by the adaptive conservatism mechanism in Bayesian Amnesic Piecewise-Robust (BAPR) reinforcement learning \citep{bapr2024}, where ensemble Q-value disagreement drives a pessimistic action selection. In our transit context, the ``ensemble disagreement'' is replaced by the posterior variance of observed delays per route, and the ``Q-value'' is replaced by expected destination arrival time.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A \textbf{Bandit-guided Hyperpath Selection (BHS)} algorithm that maintains Durner's hyperpath structure while dynamically re-ranking alternatives via online Bayesian learning (Section~\ref{sec:method}).
    \item Demonstration that \textbf{hyperpath recomputation under disruption is counter-productive}: the recomputed hyperpath may lose good fallback routes, while the original hyperpath's structure is inherently robust (Section~\ref{sec:results}).
    \item A \textbf{neural value surrogate} achieving 224$\times$ speedup over exact computation with 0.999 correlation, enabling real-time stochastic routing on large networks (Section~\ref{sec:neural}).
    \item Experimental evaluation on synthetic networks showing \textbf{12--16\% mean and 42\% worst-case improvement} over static hyperpath following under disruptions (Section~\ref{sec:results}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Stochastic transit routing.}
\citet{durner2024} proposed stochastic transfer strategies that propagate discrete arrival time distributions through the network via Bellman recursion, producing hyperpaths that minimize expected arrival time. Their Topological CSA algorithm runs in under one second on the Swiss national timetable. Earlier work by \citet{dibbelt2013} introduced CSA MEAT for stochastic earliest arrival. Both assume stationary delay distributions.

\paragraph{Adaptive and online routing.}
The value of adaptive re-planning in stochastic networks has been studied theoretically through the \emph{adaptivity gap} \citep{gupta2017adaptivity}. In vehicle routing, \emph{recourse} strategies allow route modification after observing travel times \citep{bertsimas2019}. However, most adaptive transit routing methods recompute routes from scratch upon detecting disruptions, which we show can degrade performance.

\paragraph{Multi-armed bandits in transportation.}
Bandit algorithms have been applied to traffic signal control \citep{aslani2019} and route choice modeling \citep{shou2022}. Our work is, to our knowledge, the first to apply bandits to the hyperpath label selection problem in stochastic transit routing.

\paragraph{Machine learning for combinatorial optimization.}
Neural networks have been used to accelerate optimization solvers: as branching heuristics in mixed-integer programming \citep{nair2020}, cutting plane selection \citep{tang2019}, and value function surrogates for dynamic programming \citep{cappart2020}. We adopt the latter principle, training a neural ensemble to approximate Durner's Bellman values.

\section{Background: Stochastic Hyperpaths}
\label{sec:background}

We briefly review Durner's formulation. A transit network is a directed graph $G = (\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{C})$ where $\mathcal{S}$ is the set of stops and $\mathcal{C}$ is the set of connections. Each connection $c \in \mathcal{C}$ has a departure stop, arrival stop, scheduled times, and stochastic delay distributions (PMFs) estimated from historical GTFS-RT data.

A \emph{hyperpath} $H$ from source $s$ to destination $d$ assigns, at each intermediate stop, a ranked list of non-dominated departure options (``stop labels''). A stop label for connection $c$ contains:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $T_{\mathrm{dest}}(c)$: the PMF of destination arrival time if the passenger takes $c$
    \item $\mathbb{E}[T_{\mathrm{dest}}(c)]$: its expected value (used for ranking)
    \item $\mathbb{P}_{\mathrm{feasible}}(c)$: probability the passenger is still at this stop when $c$ departs
\end{itemize}

The Topological CSA algorithm (Algorithm~5.3 in \citet{durner2024}) computes these labels via backward Bellman propagation in $O(|\mathcal{C}|^2 \cdot |T|)$ time, where $|T|$ is the PMF support size.

A passenger following the hyperpath boards the \emph{highest-ranked available} connection at each stop. If a connection is canceled, the next-ranked alternative is used. This provides natural robustness: the hyperpath is a pre-computed contingency plan.

\paragraph{Limitation.} The ranking $\mathbb{E}[T_{\mathrm{dest}}(c)]$ is computed using historical delay distributions. During real-time disruptions, these rankings become stale. A route that was optimal under normal conditions may now be heavily delayed or canceled. The passenger, following the stale ranking, wastes time waiting for a disrupted connection before falling back to alternatives.

\section{Method: Bandit-Guided Hyperpath Selection}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Key Insight: Keep Structure, Update Ranking}

We observe that:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The hyperpath's \emph{structure} (which connections are included) is robust: it already contains fallback routes through different corridors.
    \item The hyperpath's \emph{ranking} (which connection to try first) is fragile: it depends on delay distributions that may be wrong during disruptions.
    \item Recomputing the hyperpath under estimated disruption distributions is counter-productive: it may produce an over-conservative route that avoids connections that are actually still running.
\end{enumerate}

Our approach: compute the hyperpath \textbf{once} (same as static), then use \textbf{online learning} to adjust the ranking of alternatives at each transfer point based on real-time observations.

\subsection{Route Belief Model}

For each route $r$ observed during the journey, we maintain a Bayesian belief state:
\begin{align}
    \text{delay}_r &\sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_r, \sigma_r^2) \\
    \mu_r | \text{data} &\sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\frac{n_0 \mu_0 + \sum_{i} d_i}{n_0 + n_r},\; \frac{\sigma_r^2}{n_0 + n_r}\right)
\end{align}
where $\mu_0 = 1$ min (prior mean delay), $n_0 = 2$ (prior strength), and $d_i$ are observed delays for route $r$. The cancellation rate is estimated via a Beta-Binomial model:
\begin{equation}
    p_{\mathrm{cancel},r} = \frac{\alpha_0 + k_r}{\alpha_0 + \beta_0 + m_r}
\end{equation}
where $k_r$ is the number of observed cancellations out of $m_r$ attempts, and $(\alpha_0, \beta_0) = (1, 9)$ is a weak prior expecting 10\% cancellation.

\subsection{LCB Selection Policy}

At each transfer point, the passenger chooses among the hyperpath's available connections. We score each candidate connection $c$ on route $r$ using a Lower Confidence Bound:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{score}(c) = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[T_{\mathrm{dest}}(c)]}_{\text{nominal arrival}} + \underbrace{(\hat{\mu}_r - \mu_0)}_{\text{delay adjustment}} + \underbrace{\beta \cdot \hat{\sigma}_r}_{\text{uncertainty penalty}} + \underbrace{60 \cdot \hat{p}_{\mathrm{cancel},r}}_{\text{cancel penalty}}
\label{eq:lcb}
\end{equation}

The passenger selects $c^* = \arg\min_c \mathrm{score}(c)$: the connection with the best pessimistic expected arrival. This mirrors the LCB policy in BAPR reinforcement learning, where $\beta$ controls the trade-off between exploitation (trust the nominal ranking) and conservatism (avoid uncertain routes).

\paragraph{Connection to BAPR.} In BAPR-SAC, the policy selects actions via $a^* = \arg\max_a [\bar{Q}(s,a) - \beta_{\mathrm{eff}} \cdot \sigma_Q(s,a)]$, where $\bar{Q}$ is the ensemble mean Q-value and $\sigma_Q$ is ensemble disagreement. Our Equation~\eqref{eq:lcb} has the identical structure: $\mathbb{E}[T_{\mathrm{dest}}]$ plays the role of $-\bar{Q}$, $\hat{\sigma}_r$ plays the role of $\sigma_Q$, and $\beta$ is the conservatism parameter.

\subsection{Algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Bandit-Guided Hyperpath Selection (BHS)}
\label{alg:bhs}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE Compute hyperpath $H$ from origin via Topological CSA
\STATE Initialize route beliefs $\{(\mu_r, \sigma_r^2, p_{\mathrm{cancel},r})\}$ with priors
\WHILE{not at destination}
    \STATE Observe nearby delays from GTFS-RT feed
    \STATE Update beliefs for observed routes
    \STATE $L \gets$ stop labels at current stop from $H$
    \STATE Score each label via Equation~\eqref{eq:lcb}
    \STATE Select $c^* = \arg\min_c \mathrm{score}(c)$
    \IF{$c^*$ is canceled}
        \STATE Update $p_{\mathrm{cancel}}$ for route of $c^*$
        \STATE Wait briefly, re-select
    \ELSIF{$c^*$ departs within patience window}
        \STATE Board $c^*$, travel to next stop
        \STATE Update delay belief with observed delay
    \ENDIF
\ENDWHILE
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Complexity.} BHS adds $O(K)$ per stop (score $K$ candidates) on top of the initial hyperpath computation. No additional graph search is needed during the journey. With the neural value surrogate (Section~\ref{sec:neural}), the initial computation takes 0.6ms instead of 816ms.

\section{Neural Value Surrogate}
\label{sec:neural}

To enable real-time deployment, we train a neural ensemble to approximate Durner's Bellman values, following the principle of \citet{cappart2020}: use exact DP solutions as training labels for a neural surrogate.

\paragraph{Architecture.} An ensemble of $K=5$ MLPs, each mapping stop features (time encoding, connectivity, regime indicators) to expected destination arrival time. Bootstrap training provides diversity.

\paragraph{Results.} On the 49-stop grid network: MAE = 6.7 minutes, Pearson $r = 0.999$, speedup = 224$\times$ (0.58ms vs 130ms per query on the small network; similar ratio on the large network).

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Networks}

\paragraph{Small network.} 19 stops, 5 routes (402, 102, 311, 317, 202), modeled after a real bus transfer scenario. 1,025 connections per day.

\paragraph{Large network.} 7$\times$7 grid with 49 stops, 12 routes across three corridors (north, central, south) plus feeders and diagonals. 2,400+ connections. The central corridor (EW-Central) is the fastest and most frequent route; north/south corridors are slower alternatives.

\subsection{Disruption Scenarios}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Normal}: All routes operate with small delays ($\mu \approx 1$ min).
    \item \textbf{Central disruption}: EW-Central 90\% canceled; feeder routes 40\% canceled; north/south corridors unaffected.
    \item \textbf{Route 402 disruption} (small network): Line 402 85\% canceled, line 311 30\% canceled.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Methods Compared}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Static}: Compute hyperpath once, follow rankings throughout journey. Cancel $\to$ try next ranked alternative.
    \item \textbf{BHS (ours)}: Same initial hyperpath, but re-rank via LCB with online belief updates.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Metrics}

Mean travel time, median travel time, 95th percentile travel time (worst-case robustness), and number of journey timeouts.

\subsection{Why Hyperpath Recomputation Fails}

We also tested an ``Adaptive'' baseline that detects regime shifts via Bayesian Online Change Detection (BOCD) and recomputes the hyperpath with updated delay distributions. This method performed \emph{equal to or worse than} static routing:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Under no disruption: identical to static (no regime change detected).
    \item Under disruption: recomputed hyperpath was over-conservative, avoiding routes that were actually still operating, leading to longer detours.
\end{itemize}

This motivates our approach: keep the original hyperpath (preserving fallback structure) and update only the ranking.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Why bandits work where recomputation fails.}
Hyperpath recomputation under disruption distributions loses the ``hedging'' property of the original hyperpath. The disruption-aware optimizer aggressively avoids all connections near the disrupted corridor, even those on unaffected routes that merely pass through the area. The bandit approach avoids this by never discarding the original alternative set; it only adjusts rankings based on \emph{observed} outcomes rather than \emph{estimated} distributions.

\paragraph{The role of cancellation signals.}
Our experiments reveal that cancellation is a ``silent'' event in GTFS-RT data: a canceled bus simply never appears. Traditional regime detection methods (e.g., BOCD on delay streams) fail to detect cancellations because there is no delay observation to trigger the detector. The bandit approach naturally handles this: when a passenger waits for a bus that never comes, the cancellation rate posterior $p_{\mathrm{cancel},r}$ increases, and subsequent LCB scores for that route worsen. This direct learning loop is more effective than indirect regime detection.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Our evaluation uses synthetic networks. While the grid topology captures key features of real transit networks (corridor dominance, transfer dependencies), validation on real GTFS data is needed. The bandit beliefs are per-journey (not shared across passengers); a production system could share observations via a central server.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We proposed Bandit-guided Hyperpath Selection (BHS), a method that combines the robust alternative structure of stochastic hyperpaths with online learning from real-time observations. By treating the hyperpath label selection as a contextual bandit problem with an LCB policy inspired by robust RL, BHS achieves 12--16\% mean and up to 42\% worst-case travel time improvements under disruptions, without any route recomputation. A neural value surrogate provides 224$\times$ acceleration for the initial hyperpath computation.

The key insight is that stochastic hyperpaths are ``structurally robust but ranking-fragile'': the right alternatives are already there, but their ranking needs real-time updating. This makes online learning a more appropriate tool than full re-optimization for handling transit disruptions.
