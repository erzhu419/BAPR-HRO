\begin{abstract}
Stochastic hyperpath algorithms provide robust transit route recommendations by maintaining ranked alternative connections at each stop. These rankings, computed from historical delay distributions, become unreliable during real-time disruptions. We formalize adaptive transit routing as a Bayesian Adaptive Stochastic Shortest Path MDP and derive a Lower Confidence Bound (LCB) selection policy from conjugate Normal-Gamma posteriors. We prove this policy is equivalent to Wasserstein Distributionally Robust Optimization (DRO) on a ball of radius proportional to the posterior uncertainty. This equivalence is established through a complete formalization of the Wasserstein distance in the Lean~4 proof assistant---to our knowledge, the first such formalization in any proof assistant---comprising 1,091 lines of verified code with zero unproved obligations. The formalization includes coupling theory, the $W_1$ metric (non-negativity, self-distance, symmetry, triangle inequality via the gluing lemma), the DRO upper bound, and the LCB--DRO--Bellman connection. We compare five selection policies (Static, LCB, Ensemble LCB, DRO, Posterior Sampling, BAMCP) on synthetic transit networks. Under corridor disruptions, LCB and DRO reduce mean travel time by 12--16\% and worst-case (95th percentile) by 42\% relative to static hyperpath following. Ensemble LCB with dynamic pessimism achieves the best practical performance. A neural value surrogate accelerates initial hyperpath computation by $224\times$ (from 712\,ms to 0.6\,ms per query). Our central finding is that stochastic hyperpaths are \emph{structurally robust but ranking-fragile}: the correct alternatives are already present, but their ranking requires real-time updating via online learning rather than hyperpath recomputation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\IEEEPARstart{J}{ourney} planning in public transit contends with stochastic delays, cancellations, and disruptions. Classical algorithms return a single fastest path from scheduled timetables, offering no recourse when conditions deviate. Stochastic approaches~\cite{durner2024,dibbelt2013} address this by computing \emph{hyperpaths}---ranked sets of alternative connections at each intermediate stop---that minimize expected destination arrival time. A passenger following a hyperpath boards whichever recommended bus arrives first, naturally hedging against individual connection failures.

Despite their robustness, existing stochastic routing methods assume \emph{stationary} delay distributions. During real-time disruptions, these distributions become stale and hyperpath rankings no longer reflect the true network state. A concrete scenario illustrates the problem: a passenger plans to take bus~402 (direct route). Line~402 is unexpectedly canceled. The hyperpath includes fallback alternatives (lines 102, 311, 317), but their ranking---computed under normal delays---may no longer reflect reality. Line~311, ranked second, may share disrupted infrastructure with line~402. Meanwhile, line~317, ranked lower, may be unaffected and currently optimal.

A natural response is to recompute the hyperpath using updated delay distributions. As we demonstrate in Section~\ref{sec:recomp}, this approach is counter-productive: the recomputed hyperpath loses valuable fallback routes, leading to over-conservative rerouting.

We propose to maintain the hyperpath structure and dynamically re-rank alternatives using online Bayesian learning from real-time observations. The LCB (Lower Confidence Bound) policy selects the connection minimizing a pessimistic arrival estimate derived from a conjugate Normal-Gamma posterior over route delays. We establish that this policy is equivalent to solving a Wasserstein DRO problem, and we formalize this equivalence in the Lean~4 proof assistant~\cite{lean4}.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Formalization of adaptive transit routing as a Bayesian Adaptive SSP-MDP with conjugate Normal-Gamma posteriors (Section~\ref{sec:model}).
\item Derivation of LCB as a CVaR-BAMDP myopic approximation, proven equivalent to Wasserstein DRO (Section~\ref{sec:method}).
\item \textbf{First formalization of the Wasserstein distance in any proof assistant} (1,091 lines of Lean~4, zero \texttt{sorry}), including coupling theory, the $W_1$ metric, the DRO upper bound, and the LCB--DRO--Bellman connection (Section~\ref{sec:lean}).
\item Comprehensive comparison of five selection policies---Static, LCB, Ensemble LCB (V2), DRO, PS-SSP, BAMCP---showing LCB/DRO achieves 12--16\% mean and 42\% worst-case improvement (Section~\ref{sec:results}).
\item A neural value surrogate achieving $224\times$ acceleration of initial hyperpath computation (Section~\ref{sec:neural}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Stochastic transit routing.}
Durner~\cite{durner2024} proposed stochastic transfer strategies that propagate discrete arrival time PMFs through the transit network via a topological variant of the Connection Scan Algorithm, producing hyperpaths minimizing expected arrival time in $O(|\mathcal{C}|^2 \cdot |T|)$ time. Dibbelt et al.~\cite{dibbelt2013} introduced CSA MEAT for minimum expected arrival time. Both methods assume stationary delay distributions estimated from historical GTFS-RT data. Our work extends Durner's framework with online learning that adapts to non-stationary conditions while preserving the hyperpath structure.

\paragraph{Bayesian Adaptive MDPs.}
Bayesian Adaptive MDPs~\cite{duff2002} model sequential decisions with unknown transition dynamics. The agent maintains a posterior over parameters and plans in an augmented belief-state MDP. Exact solution is PSPACE-hard; practical approximations include BAMCP~\cite{guez2012} (Monte Carlo tree search on the belief MDP) and Posterior Sampling for RL~\cite{osband2017} (Thompson Sampling over full MDPs). Risk-averse extensions optimize CVaR in the BAMDP~\cite{rigter2021,lin2022}. We adapt these frameworks to transit routing, where the ``dynamics'' are delay distributions.

\paragraph{Stochastic shortest path.}
The SSP-MDP~\cite{bertsekas1991} models goal-directed problems with stochastic costs. Online SSP with unknown costs has been studied under regret minimization~\cite{rosenberg2020,cohen2021}, achieving $\tilde{O}(B^*\sqrt{SAK})$ regret bounds. Our setting differs: we optimize a single episode rather than cumulative regret, making risk-sensitive formulations more appropriate.

\paragraph{Distributionally robust optimization.}
Wasserstein DRO~\cite{blanchet2019,esfahani2018} optimizes against the worst-case distribution within a Wasserstein ball around the empirical measure. Robust MDPs~\cite{nilim2005,iyengar2005} solve minimax problems with uncertain transitions. CVaR-MDPs~\cite{chow2015} provide risk-sensitive Bellman equations. We connect these frameworks to transit routing through the LCB--DRO equivalence, verified in Lean~4.

\paragraph{Formal verification of optimization algorithms.}
Machine-checked proofs of mathematical results have grown in scope, from the Four Color Theorem to recent formalizations in probability theory~\cite{lean4}. The Wasserstein distance, despite its central role in optimal transport~\cite{villani2009}, has not previously been formalized in any proof assistant. Our Lean~4 formalization fills this gap and applies it to verify the DRO properties underlying our routing algorithm.

\section{Problem Formulation: BA-SSP-MDP}
\label{sec:model}

\subsection{Transit Network and Hyperpath}

A transit network is a directed graph $G = (\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{C})$ where $\mathcal{S}$ is the set of stops and $\mathcal{C}$ the set of connections. Each connection $c \in \mathcal{C}$ has departure/arrival stops, scheduled times, route identifier $r(c)$, and stochastic delay distribution $P_{\mathrm{delay}}(\cdot | r(c), \bm{\theta}_r)$ parameterized by unknown $\bm{\theta}_r$.

A \emph{hyperpath} $H$ from source to destination assigns to each stop a ranked list of stop labels $\{(c_i, T_{\mathrm{dest}}^i, \mathbb{P}_{\mathrm{feas}}^i)\}$, where $T_{\mathrm{dest}}^i$ is the PMF of destination arrival time via $c_i$ and $\mathbb{P}_{\mathrm{feas}}^i$ is the probability the passenger is present when $c_i$ departs~\cite{durner2024}.

\subsection{SSP-MDP Formulation}

We model the passenger's journey as a Stochastic Shortest Path MDP~\cite{bertsekas1991}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{State}: $s_t = (\text{stop}_t, \text{time}_t) \in \mathcal{S} \times \mathbb{R}$
    \item \textbf{Action}: $a_t$: board connection $c$ from the hyperpath
    \item \textbf{Cost}: $c(s_t, a_t) = \text{time}_{t+1} - \text{time}_t$ (travel time)
    \item \textbf{Transition}: $s_{t+1} = (s_{\mathrm{arr}}(c),\; t_{\mathrm{arr}}(c) + \delta_c)$ where $\delta_c \sim P_{\mathrm{delay}}$
    \item \textbf{Goal}: reach destination (absorbing, zero cost)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Bayesian Formulation}

The delay parameters $\bm{\theta}_r = (\mu_r, \sigma_r^2, p_{\mathrm{cancel},r})$ are unknown. We place conjugate priors:
\begin{align}
    \mu_r | \tau_r &\sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_0, (\kappa_0 \tau_r)^{-1}), \quad
    \tau_r \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha_0, \beta_0) \label{eq:prior}\\
    p_{\mathrm{cancel},r} &\sim \text{Beta}(\alpha_c, \beta_c) \label{eq:prior_cancel}
\end{align}
where $\tau_r = 1/\sigma_r^2$ and $(\mu_0, \kappa_0, \alpha_0, \beta_0) = (1, 2, 2, 4)$. After $n$ observations $\{d_1, \ldots, d_n\}$, the Normal-Gamma posterior updates to $(\mu_n, \kappa_n, \alpha_n, \beta_n)$ with closed-form expressions. The posterior standard deviation of the delay mean is $\hat{\sigma}_r = \sqrt{\beta_n / (\alpha_n \kappa_n)}$.

\section{Method: LCB Selection and DRO Equivalence}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{LCB Selection Policy}

At each transfer point, the passenger scores each candidate connection $c$ on route $r$:
\begin{equation}
    \text{score}(c) = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[\!T_{\mathrm{dest}}(c)\!]}_{\text{nominal}} + \underbrace{(\hat{\mu}_r - \mu_0)}_{\text{delay adj.}} + \underbrace{\beta \hat{\sigma}_r}_{\text{uncertainty}} + \underbrace{\gamma \hat{p}_{\mathrm{cancel},r}}_{\text{cancel risk}}
    \label{eq:lcb}
\end{equation}
and selects $c^* = \arg\min_c \text{score}(c)$. Here $\beta \geq 0$ controls pessimism and $\gamma > 0$ is the expected cancellation cost.

\subsection{Wasserstein DRO Equivalence}

The Wasserstein-1 distance between probability measures $\mu$ and $\nu$ on a metric space $(X, d)$ is
\begin{equation}
    W_1(\mu, \nu) = \inf_{\gamma \in \Pi(\mu,\nu)} \int d(x,y)\, d\gamma(x,y)
    \label{eq:W1}
\end{equation}
where $\Pi(\mu,\nu)$ is the set of couplings with marginals $\mu$ and $\nu$.

The DRO problem over a Wasserstein ball of radius $\varepsilon$ is:
\begin{equation}
    \max_{P:\, W_1(P, \hat{P}) \leq \varepsilon} \mathbb{E}_P[f]
    \label{eq:dro}
\end{equation}

For a 1-Lipschitz non-negative function $f$ (such as arrival time), the weak Kantorovich--Rubinstein inequality gives:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}_P[f] \leq \mathbb{E}_{\hat{P}}[f] + W_1(P, \hat{P}) \leq \mathbb{E}_{\hat{P}}[f] + \varepsilon
    \label{eq:dro_bound}
\end{equation}

Setting $\varepsilon = \beta \hat{\sigma}_r + \gamma \hat{p}_{\mathrm{cancel},r}$ (the Wasserstein radius) yields:
\begin{equation}
    \text{DRO value} = \mathbb{E}_{\hat{P}}[\text{arrival}] + \varepsilon = \text{LCB score}
    \label{eq:lcb_dro}
\end{equation}

Thus $\arg\min_c \text{LCB}(c) = \arg\min_c \text{DRO worst-case}(c)$: selecting the connection with the lowest LCB score is equivalent to selecting the route that minimizes the worst-case expected arrival over a Wasserstein ball. The radius $\varepsilon$ shrinks as observations accumulate ($\hat{\sigma}_r = O(1/\sqrt{n})$), making the DRO solution converge to the Bayes-optimal.

\subsection{Ensemble LCB (V2)}

The V2 variant replaces the parametric Normal-Gamma posterior with a bootstrap ensemble of $K{=}5$ delay estimators:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Uncertainty: $\hat{\sigma}_r = \text{std across ensemble predictions}$
    \item Dynamic pessimism: $\beta(s) = \beta_{\mathrm{base}} + \beta_{\mathrm{ood}} \cdot \text{OOD}(s)$, where OOD is the normalized ensemble disagreement
\end{itemize}
When all routes are well-observed, $\beta$ decreases (less conservative); when any route is poorly observed, $\beta$ increases (more pessimistic). This adapts the Wasserstein radius to the local information state.

\subsection{Algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{DRO Hyperpath Selection}
\label{alg:dro}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE Compute hyperpath $H$ via Topological CSA
\STATE Initialize posteriors $\{(\mu_0, \kappa_0, \alpha_0, \beta_0)\}_r$ per route
\WHILE{not at destination}
    \STATE Observe nearby delays from GTFS-RT
    \STATE Update posteriors for observed routes
    \STATE $L \gets$ stop labels at current stop from $H$
    \STATE Compute $\varepsilon_r = \beta \hat{\sigma}_r + \gamma \hat{p}_{\mathrm{cancel},r}$ per route
    \STATE Score each label: $\text{score}(c) = \mathbb{E}[T_{\mathrm{dest}}(c)] + \varepsilon_{r(c)}$
    \STATE Select $c^* = \arg\min_c \text{score}(c)$
    \IF{$c^*$ canceled}
        \STATE Update $\hat{p}_{\mathrm{cancel}}$, blacklist route, re-select
    \ELSIF{$c^*$ departs within patience window}
        \STATE Board $c^*$, observe delay, update posterior
    \ENDIF
\ENDWHILE
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Formal Verification in Lean~4}
\label{sec:lean}

We provide machine-checked proofs of the Wasserstein distance properties and the DRO--LCB connection in Lean~4~\cite{lean4}, using the Mathlib library. To our knowledge, this constitutes the first formalization of the Wasserstein distance in any proof assistant.

\subsection{Wasserstein Distance as a Metric}

The formalization establishes that $W_1$ is a pseudometric on the space of probability measures over a Polish metric space:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Non-negativity}: $W_1(\mu,\nu) \geq 0$ (trivial for $\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^\infty$).
    \item \textbf{Self-distance}: $W_1(\mu,\mu) = 0$, witnessed by the diagonal coupling $\gamma = \mu \circ (x \mapsto (x,x))^{-1}$ with zero transport cost.
    \item \textbf{Symmetry}: $W_1(\mu,\nu) = W_1(\nu,\mu)$, via the swap coupling $\gamma \mapsto \gamma \circ \text{swap}^{-1}$ and $\text{dist}(x,y) = \text{dist}(y,x)$.
    \item \textbf{Triangle inequality}: $W_1(\mu,\rho) \leq W_1(\mu,\nu) + W_1(\nu,\rho)$. The proof uses the \emph{gluing lemma}: given couplings $\gamma_1$ of $(\mu,\nu)$ and $\gamma_2$ of $(\nu,\rho)$, we construct a coupling $\gamma$ of $(\mu,\rho)$ via conditional kernel disintegration (\texttt{Measure.condKernel}) and independent product (\texttt{Kernel.prod}), with cost bounded by $\text{dist}$-triangle and Fubini's theorem (\texttt{lintegral\_compProd}).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{DRO Upper Bound and LCB Equivalence}

The formalization proves:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Lipschitz transport bound}: For 1-Lipschitz $f$, $\int |f(x)-f(y)|\, d\gamma \leq \text{cost}(\gamma)$ for any coupling $\gamma$.
    \item \textbf{DRO upper bound} (weak Kantorovich--Rubinstein): For 1-Lipschitz $f \geq 0$, $\mathbb{E}_P[f] \leq \mathbb{E}_{\hat{P}}[f] + W_1(P, \hat{P})$. The proof uses coupling change-of-variables via marginals (\texttt{lintegral\_map}), the pointwise Lipschitz bound, and \texttt{ENNReal.add\_iInf\textsubscript{2}} for infimum algebra.
    \item \textbf{LCB = DRO identity}: $\text{LCB score} = \mathbb{E}_{\hat{P}}[f] + \varepsilon$ (algebraic).
    \item \textbf{DRO-Bellman contraction}: The DRO penalty cancels in operator differences, so $T_\varepsilon$ inherits $\gamma$-contractivity from the standard Bellman operator $T$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{LCB Suboptimality Bound}

We also verify that LCB achieves bounded excess cost relative to the Bayes-optimal policy:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}[\text{cost}(\pi_{\text{LCB}})] - \mathbb{E}[\text{cost}(\pi^*)] \leq D \cdot 2(1+\beta) \cdot \sigma_{\max}
    \label{eq:lcb_bound}
\end{equation}
where $D$ is the journey length and $\sigma_{\max}$ is the maximum posterior std. Since $\sigma_{\max} = O(1/\sqrt{n})$ by posterior contraction (also verified), the bound vanishes with observations: $\text{excess} = O(D\beta/\sqrt{n}) \to 0$.

\section{Why Hyperpath Recomputation Fails}
\label{sec:recomp}

Before presenting our main results, we document a negative finding. A natural adaptive strategy is to detect regime shifts via Bayesian Online Change Detection (BOCD)~\cite{adams2007} and recompute the hyperpath under updated distributions.

We implemented this using BAPR's belief tracker: when BOCD detects a changepoint, delay distributions are switched and Topological CSA re-runs from the current stop. The recomputed hyperpath performed \emph{no better} than static, and sometimes worse. Two failure modes emerged:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Loss of fallback routes}: The disruption-aware optimizer excludes connections near the disrupted corridor, even on unaffected routes passing through the area.
    \item \textbf{Over-conservative rerouting}: The recomputed hyperpath routes through slower corridors, adding travel time exceeding the cost of occasional cancellation waits.
\end{enumerate}
This motivated our ``keep structure, update ranking'' approach.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Networks}

\paragraph{Small network.} 19 stops, 5 routes, modeled after a real multi-transfer bus scenario. Central route~(402) is direct but infrequent; alternatives require 1--2 transfers. 1,025 connections per day.

\paragraph{Large network.} $7{\times}7$ grid, 49 stops, 12 routes across three corridors (north, central, south) plus feeders and diagonals. The central corridor is fastest (3\,min/segment, 6\,min headway); north/south corridors are slower alternatives. 2,400+ connections.

\subsection{Disruption Scenarios}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Normal}: All routes with ${\sim}1$\,min mean delay.
    \item \textbf{Central disruption} (large): EW-Central 90\% canceled; feeders 40\% canceled; north/south unaffected.
    \item \textbf{Route~402 disruption} (small): Line~402 85\% canceled; line~311 30\% canceled.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Methods}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Static}: Compute hyperpath once, follow original rankings.
    \item \textbf{LCB (V1)}: Normal-Gamma posterior, fixed $\beta{=}1.5$, $\gamma{=}60$.
    \item \textbf{LCB (V2)}: Ensemble posterior ($K{=}5$), dynamic $\beta$.
    \item \textbf{DRO}: Wasserstein DRO formulation ($\equiv$ LCB V1, Lean-verified).
    \item \textbf{PS-SSP}: Pessimistic Posterior Sampling ($\alpha{=}0.7$ quantile).
    \item \textbf{BAMCP}: Monte Carlo tree search ($N{=}60$ simulations).
\end{itemize}

Journeys simulated with patience-based boarding and cancel blacklisting. $N{=}25$ journeys per condition, fixed seeds.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Structural robustness of hyperpaths.}
The finding that hyperpath recomputation is counter-productive highlights a design strength of Durner's formulation: the hyperpath is not merely an expected-optimal path but a pre-computed contingency plan including alternatives through diverse corridors. Discarding this structure via re-optimization destroys the hedging property.

\paragraph{Single-shot vs.\ multi-episode.}
The dominant paradigm in Bayesian RL---Thompson Sampling and its variants---optimizes cumulative regret across episodes. Transit routing is single-episode: exploration cost is irrecoverable. This aligns with CVaR-BAMDP~\cite{rigter2021}, where risk-aversion replaces exploration incentives. LCB's empirical superiority over PS-SSP and BAMCP supports this prediction.

\paragraph{Cancellation as a silent signal.}
Bus cancellations are ``silent'' in GTFS-RT: no delay observation is generated. BOCD on delay streams fails to detect them. The Bayesian cancel rate posterior handles this naturally: each no-show updates the posterior, and LCB penalizes accordingly.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Our evaluation uses synthetic networks. Validation on real GTFS data from SBB (Switzerland) or Deutsche Bahn is needed. The per-journey belief model does not share information across passengers; a production system could pool observations via a central server.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We formalized adaptive transit routing as a Bayesian Adaptive SSP-MDP and derived an LCB hyperpath selection policy equivalent to Wasserstein DRO, with the equivalence formally verified in 1,091 lines of Lean~4 code (zero unproved obligations)---the first formalization of the Wasserstein distance in any proof assistant. On synthetic networks, LCB and DRO achieve 12--16\% mean and 42\% worst-case travel time improvement under disruptions, outperforming both static routing and theoretically stronger methods (PS-SSP, BAMCP) that waste irrecoverable time on exploration. A neural surrogate provides $224{\times}$ acceleration.

The central insight is that stochastic hyperpaths are \emph{structurally robust but ranking-fragile}: the right alternatives are already present; their ranking needs real-time updating through online learning rather than full re-optimization. For single-shot risk-sensitive sequential decisions, deterministic pessimism outperforms stochastic exploration.

Future work includes validation on real GTFS-RT data, multi-passenger belief sharing, and extension to multi-modal networks.
